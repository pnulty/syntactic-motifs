What is a Syntactic Motif?

How the hidden structure of sentences can reveal who — or what — wrote them.

1. Sentences Have Structure

Every sentence can be broken down into a parse tree — a hierarchy of phrases, parts of speech, and words. Here's a sentence and its tree:

"It was not just a challenge, it was a revelation."

2. Extracting a Pattern

Look at the highlighted clauses in the tree above: "It was not just a challenge" and "it was a revelation." Together they form a "not just X, it was Y" construction — two parallel clauses joined by a comma.

A syntactic motif is this structural skeleton — the shape without the words:

↓
(S (S (NP (PRP)) (VP (VBD) (RB) (NP (RB) (DT) (NN)))) (,) (S (NP (PRP)) (VP (VBD) (NP (DT) (NN)))) (.))

We strip away the actual words and keep only the grammatical structure. This lets us find sentences that have the same shape, even when the words are completely different.

3. Same Pattern, Different Words

Here's the striking part. When we search a corpus of LLM-generated text for sentences matching this pattern, we find the same "not just X, it was Y" construction repeated with different words slotted in:

The words change but the structure stays the same. This particular motif — the "not just X, it was Y" rhetorical escalation — is a strong LLM writing fingerprint.

4. More LLM Fingerprints

This isn't a one-off. LLMs have a whole repertoire of structural habits. Here are two more patterns that are distinctively overused by AI:

"Noun VERBed ..., VERBing ..."

Pattern: (S (NP (NNS)) (VP (VBD) (PP) (,) (S)) (.)) — PMI +3.75, 70 occurrences
  • Shadows danced in the moonlight, taking on ethereal shapes.
  • Whispers echoed through the trees, recounting tales of vanished tribes.
  • Ideas swirled like autumn leaves, each carrying a fragment of a story.

A plural noun does something poetic, then a participial clause adds a second layer of imagery. LLMs reach for this structure constantly.

"became more ADJ" / "grew more ADJ"

Pattern: (VP (VBD) (ADJP (RBR) (JJ))) — PMI +4.26, 50 occurrences
  • The silence grew more extreme.
  • The pattern became more apparent.
  • The colors became more vibrant.
  • The situation grew more dire.

A simple verb + comparative adjective. LLMs love escalation — things always "grow more" or "become more" something.

"his voice cold and hollow" — absolute construction

Pattern: (S (NP (PRP$) (NN)) (ADJP (JJ) (CC) (JJ))) — PMI +4.37, 54 occurrences
  • his voice soft and kind
  • her voice steady and resolute
  • her gaze unyielding and unforgiving
  • its voice cold and hollow

A possessive pronoun + noun, then two adjectives joined by "and." LLMs use this absolute construction 20 times more often than human writers.

5. What Makes a Pattern Distinctive?

Not every syntactic pattern is interesting — some structures are used equally by everyone. To find distinctive patterns, we use Pointwise Mutual Information (PMI), which compares how often a pattern appears in one source versus the overall baseline:

PMI = log2( freqsource / freqbaseline )

A positive PMI means the source overuses the pattern — it appears more often than expected. A negative PMI means the source underuses it. The higher the absolute value, the more distinctive the pattern is.

LLMs tend toward ornate, layered constructions (absolute phrases, participial clauses, paired adjectives). Humans — at least in comparable fiction — use more direct, economical structures.
